The core understanding

Vision is not primarily an eye problem. It is a brain phenomenon, a nervous system phenomenon, and — most fundamentally — a vibrational phenomenon. Over 50% of the brain is dedicated to visual processing. The retina is brain tissue. Decline is not written in the structure of your eye. It is written in a responsive, dynamic system that is always listening to your dominant thoughts, beliefs, and emotional patterns.

The three leverage points
  • Vibrational — Your emotional state and dominant thoughts directly regulate the nervous system that governs focus, pupil size, lens flexibility, and retinal blood flow.
  • Physical — The eye's mitochondria-dense cones need specific nutrition, movement, light, and rest to maintain their extraordinary energy demands.
  • Habitual — Modern life has created a perfect storm for vision deterioration: excessive near-work, blue light, poor posture, chronic stress. Each habit can be gently reversed.

Your Daily Non-Negotiables — every single day

MorningUpon waking — before glasses if possible — take 3 slow breaths and say inwardly: "My eyes are whole. My vision is clear. I am becoming."
MorningBlink intentionally 10 times with a gentle 2-second close-and-squeeze. This activates the oculocardiac reflex and triggers parasympathetic focus.
Every hour20-20-20 rule — every 20 minutes at a screen, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Blink every 5 seconds while doing so.
MealsInclude one eye-nutrition food each day (see Nutrition tab). Prioritise wild fish, egg yolks, leafy greens, berries.
EveningAmber blue-blocking glasses after sunset. Warm lighting. No screens within 30 min of bed where possible.
Evening1-minute vision journal: one moment of clarity today, one thing your eyes did well, one thing you're grateful for.

Less is more — always

Eye exercises are metabolically more demanding for the brain than a heavy gym session for the body. Start with less than you think you need. Build slowly. If you feel dizzy, fatigued, or notice worsening — you've done too much. Rest is part of the practice.

Morning Practice (10 min)

Conscious Becoming Practice + intention

Full morning practice. Today during your heart coherence breathing, consciously extend the exhale to twice the length of the inhale. This is the most direct way to activate the parasympathetic nervous system — which is the same system that governs lens focus and pupil constriction.

Physical Practice (5 min)

Intentional blinking drill

Set a timer for 3 minutes. Every 10 seconds, perform one deliberate slow blink: close for 2 seconds with a gentle squeeze, then open. Notice any immediate sharpening of focus after the squeeze — this is the oculocardiac reflex at work. You are literally triggering a parasympathetic response that improves lens focusing.

Nutrition focus today: Eat wild oily fish or take omega-3 (EPA/DHA). DHA makes up 50–60% of the structural mass of your retina. This is not optional nutrition — it is retinal building material.

Day 8 — Monday
Eye movement training
TrainingVibrational
▼

Morning Practice (10 min)

Conscious Becoming Practice

Full practice. In your visualisation today — imagine moving your eyes freely, easily, with full control and precision. Effortless ocular movement is a sign of a healthy, well-supported visual brain.

Physical Practice (8 min)

Slow eye movements — head still
  • Sit upright, head completely still. Only the eyes move.
  • Up and down, slowly: 8 repetitions. The movement should take 3–4 seconds each direction.
  • Left and right, slowly: 8 repetitions.
  • Diagonal (top-left to bottom-right and reverse): 6 repetitions each diagonal.
  • Rest 1 min — palm over eyes.
  • Clockwise circle, very slowly: 4 full rotations. Then counterclockwise: 4 rotations.

Total: no more than 4 minutes of movement. This is more than enough for Day 8. The brain does not signal fatigue like muscles do — stop before you think you need to.

Day 9 — Tuesday
Near-far focus drill
TrainingNutrition
▼

Morning Practice (10 min)

Conscious Becoming Practice

Full practice. Today hold a specific image during your visualisation: looking out of a window and seeing both near (a plant on the sill) and far (a tree, a hill, the sky) with equal ease. The lens moving freely and effortlessly between distances.

Physical Practice (8 min)

Near-far focus drill
  • Hold your thumb 25cm from your face. Focus on your thumbprint — really look at the detail. Hold for 5 seconds. Feel the ciliary muscle engaging.
  • Look at a point at least 6 metres away. Focus there for 5 seconds. Feel the lens relax and flatten.
  • Alternate for 3 minutes total. Blink between each shift.
  • Rest with palming for 2 minutes.

This exercises the ciliary muscle — the muscle whose flexibility is most associated with presbyopia and near-vision changes. The key is the elasticity, not force.

Nutrition today: Egg yolks — 2–3 with breakfast. Lutein and zeaxanthin in egg yolk is significantly more bioavailable than from plant sources. These carotenoids concentrate in your macula and act as natural blue-light filters.

Posture & the neck
PostureTraining

Morning Practice (10 min)

Conscious Becoming Practice

Full practice. The second cervical vertebra (C2) is myofascially connected to the optic nerve pathway. Today's awareness: as you breathe, consciously soften and lengthen the back of your neck. Feel the optic pathway opening.

Physical Practice (10 min)

Neck mobilisation — vision locked
  • Slowly turn your head fully left — keeping your eyes fixed straight ahead. This means your eyes rotate in the opposite direction. Hold 5 seconds. Return. Right. Repeat 6 times each side.
  • Gentle lateral flexion: ear toward shoulder, slowly. 5 each side.
  • Forward flexion and gentle extension: chin toward chest, then gentle tilt back. 5 each.
  • Shoulder rolls — large, slow, backward. 10 repetitions.
  • Finish with 3 min palming.

The neurological pairing of neck and eye muscles means this mobilisation directly stimulates the visual brain. Research shows an 80%+ correlation between head posture and astigmatism angle.

Nutrition Guide

Feeding the Visual System

These are the specific nutrients your retinal cone cells, macula, and lens need to thrive. The mitochondria-dense cones are the first to suffer when nutrition is poor.

"If you can support your eyes nutritionally, you are already 80% of the way there in terms of supporting your eyes for a lifetime of healthy eyesight."

— Dr. Ranny Banck, Integrative Ophthalmologist
DHA / EPA (Omega-3)
Wild salmon, sardines, mackerel, cod liver oil. DHA = 50–60% of retinal structural mass. Non-negotiable.
Lutein & Zeaxanthin
Egg yolks (most bioavailable), kale, spinach, Swiss chard, broccoli. Concentrate in the macula. Natural blue-light filters.
Vitamin A (preformed)
Beef liver, cod liver oil, butter, egg yolks. Supports cornea and retina. More bioavailable than beta-carotene.
Magnesium (chelated)
Leafy greens, pumpkin seeds, dark chocolate. Supplement chelated form. Supports mitochondria, reduces stress, relaxes ciliary muscle.
Zinc
Pumpkin seeds, lamb, beef, oysters. Enables vitamin A to function in the retina. Supports night vision.
Anthocyanins
Blueberries, blackcurrants, blackberries. Strengthen retinal capillaries. Improve night vision. Anti-inflammatory.
Vitamin D3 + K2
Sunlight (primary), cod liver oil, fatty fish. D3 is anti-inflammatory; research shows high-dose D3 can reduce intraocular pressure. Always take with K2 and magnesium.
Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)
Pork, nutritional yeast, sunflower seeds. Deficiency causes dry eye via autonomic nervous system dysfunction. Depleted by excess carbs, coffee, alcohol.
Saffron
Spice form or supplement. Specific research on macular degeneration — improves visual acuity in AMD. Add to food or take as supplement.
Carnosine / NAC drops
Red meat (carnosine). N-acetyl carnosine eye drops for cataracts — dissolves glycated proteins in the lens. Available OTC.
What to reduce or eliminate
  • Refined sugar and high-starch foods — directly linked to cataracts, retinopathy, and macular degeneration via glycation (sugar bonding to proteins).
  • Ultra-processed foods — displace nutrients, drive inflammation, impair mitochondrial function.
  • Excess alcohol and coffee — both deplete B1, which affects tear production and autonomic eye function.
  • Blue light after sunset — disrupts circadian rhythm, melatonin production, and nightly retinal repair.
